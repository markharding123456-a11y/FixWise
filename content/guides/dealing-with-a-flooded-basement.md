## Safety First

Before you wade in, stop and think about safety. Flooded basements can be dangerous.

- **Electrical hazard.** If water is touching any electrical outlet, appliance, or your electrical panel — do NOT enter the water. Call an electrician first, or have your power company disconnect power to the basement.
- **Sewage risk.** If the flood came from a sewer backup, the water contains bacteria and waste. Wear rubber boots, gloves, and avoid skin contact.
- **Structural risk.** If the water came in through a foundation wall crack under pressure, the wall may be compromised. Look for bowing or new cracks before entering.

> **Critical:** If there's any chance the water is in contact with electrical sources, stay out and call a professional. This is not worth the risk.

## The First Hour: Stop the Source

### If it's a burst pipe:
Find your main water shut-off valve and turn it off. This is usually near your water meter, often in the basement (which may be flooded) or where the main water line enters your house.

### If it's sewer backup:
Don't run any water or flush toilets in the house. The backup will only get worse. Call a plumber.

### If it's groundwater or rain:
You can't stop it at the source, but you can start removing the water. Check your sump pump — if you have one and it's not running, it may need to be reset or replaced.

## The First 24 Hours: Remove the Water

Time matters. The longer water sits, the worse the damage.

### Start extracting water
- Use a wet/dry shop vacuum for manageable amounts
- For significant flooding (several inches or more), rent or buy a submersible pump
- Pump water away from the foundation — at least 10 feet from the house

### Document everything
Before you move or throw away anything, take photos and video of the damage from multiple angles. Your insurance company will need this.

### Move what you can save
Get furniture, boxes, and valuables out of the water and into a dry area. Anything that's been submerged for more than 48 hours in contaminated water is likely a loss.

### Start air movement
Once the standing water is out, get fans and a dehumidifier running immediately. Open windows if weather permits. Air movement is your best friend for drying.

## Days 2-7: Dry Everything

This is where most homeowners underestimate the job. Water gets into everything — behind walls, under floors, inside furniture, into insulation. Just because the floor looks dry doesn't mean the structure is dry.

### What needs to come out:
- **Carpet and underpad** — Carpet can sometimes be saved if cleaned within 48 hours of clean water exposure. Underpad is always a loss — it's a sponge.
- **Wet drywall** — Cut drywall at least 12 inches above the water line. Water wicks upward through drywall.
- **Wet insulation** — Fiberglass batts that got soaked need to come out. They won't dry properly in the wall cavity.
- **Baseboards** — Remove them to let the wall cavity dry behind.

### Monitor moisture levels
If you have a moisture meter (about $30 at hardware stores), check the moisture in studs and subfloor daily. You want readings below 15% before closing up walls.

### Run dehumidifiers 24/7
Set them to drain continuously if possible (many have a hose connection). Empty the bucket regularly if not. You want relative humidity below 50%.

## Preventing Mold

Mold can start growing within 24-48 hours of water exposure. Speed is everything.

- **Spray exposed framing** with an antimicrobial product after removing wet materials
- **Keep air moving** — stagnant, humid air is mold's best friend
- **Don't close up walls** until everything reads dry. Sealing in moisture guarantees mold
- **Watch for musty smells** in the weeks after — that's your early warning

## Insurance Tips

- **Call your insurance company immediately.** Don't wait until cleanup is done.
- **Keep receipts** for everything you spend on cleanup and equipment rental
- **Don't throw away damaged items** until the adjuster has seen them or you have thorough documentation
- **Know your policy.** Many basic policies don't cover sewer backup or overland flooding — these are usually add-ons.

## When to Call a Pro

Honestly? Almost always with a flooded basement. This guide helps you manage the first 24 hours and prevent further damage, but proper restoration requires:

- **Professional water extraction equipment** — industrial pumps and extractors are 10x faster than consumer equipment
- **Moisture mapping** — pros use thermal cameras and pin meters to find hidden moisture
- **Structural drying** — professional dehumidifiers and air movers are significantly more powerful than consumer units
- **Antimicrobial treatment** — proper application prevents mold growth
- **Documentation for insurance** — restoration companies provide detailed moisture logs that support your claim

A restoration company can often have equipment on-site within hours. The cost of professional drying is almost always less than the cost of mold remediation later.
