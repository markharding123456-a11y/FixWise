## Before You Rip Anything Out

The biggest mistake in bathroom renovations is starting demolition before finishing the plan. A proper plan prevents budget overruns, change orders, and the dreaded half-finished bathroom that stays that way for months.

## Step 1: Define the Scope

Be honest about what you're actually trying to accomplish:

- **Cosmetic refresh** — New paint, fixtures, mirror, lighting. Budget: $500-2,000. Mostly DIY-friendly.
- **Surface update** — New flooring, vanity, toilet, tub surround. Budget: $2,000-8,000. Mix of DIY and pro work.
- **Full renovation** — Gut and redo everything including plumbing and electrical. Budget: $8,000-25,000+. Mostly pro work.

> **Pro Tip:** Scope creep is the #1 budget killer in bathroom renos. Write down exactly what you want to change and stick to the list. "While we're at it" is the most expensive phrase in renovation.

## Step 2: Set a Realistic Budget

Whatever number you have in your head, add 20% for surprises. Old bathrooms hide problems behind walls — rotten subfloor, corroded pipes, inadequate venting.

### Budget breakdown for a typical mid-range reno:
- **Labor:** 40-50% of total (plumbing, electrical, tile, finishing)
- **Materials:** 30-40% (tile, vanity, fixtures, toilet, lighting)
- **Fixtures and fittings:** 10-15% (faucets, showerhead, hardware)
- **Contingency:** 10-20% (the stuff you don't know about yet)

### Where to save:
- Keep plumbing in the same locations (moving a toilet costs $1,000+ just for the plumbing)
- Refinish the tub instead of replacing it ($300 vs. $1,500+)
- Use standard-size vanities instead of custom
- Shop sales for tile — last year's styles are deeply discounted

### Where not to cut corners:
- **Waterproofing.** A properly waterproofed shower costs $200-400 in materials. A failed shower pan costs $5,000+ in water damage.
- **Ventilation.** A good exhaust fan prevents mold. Don't skip it.
- **Plumbing.** If the pipes are old, replace them while the walls are open. You won't want to do it later.

## Step 3: Measure Everything

Before you shop for anything, measure your bathroom precisely:

- Room dimensions (length, width, height)
- Door and window locations and sizes
- Distance from walls to drain centers (toilet, tub, sink)
- Location of existing electrical outlets and switches
- Ceiling height
- Any obstacles (ducts, pipes, structural columns)

Draw a floor plan on graph paper or use a free app. This prevents the incredibly frustrating experience of buying a vanity that doesn't fit.

## Step 4: Decide What to DIY vs. Hire Out

### Good DIY projects:
- Painting
- Installing a new toilet (it's easier than people think)
- Replacing a vanity (if plumbing connections stay the same)
- Installing new light fixtures (turn off the breaker first)
- Replacing hardware (towel bars, toilet paper holder, hooks)
- Installing a new mirror

### Hire a professional for:
- **Moving plumbing** — Requires permits and inspection in most jurisdictions
- **Electrical work** — New circuits, GFCI outlets, fan installation. Code requires licensed work in many areas.
- **Tile work** — Shower tile is not beginner-friendly. Floor tile is more forgiving.
- **Waterproofing** — The shower pan membrane or liquid waterproofing must be done right. One mistake and you'll be redoing the entire project.

## Step 5: Plan the Order of Work

Bathroom renos must happen in a specific sequence:

1. **Demolition** — Remove old fixtures, tile, drywall as needed
2. **Rough plumbing** — Move or add supply and drain lines
3. **Rough electrical** — Move or add circuits, boxes, fan wiring
4. **Inspection** (if required by your local code)
5. **Waterproofing** — Shower pan, wall membrane, floor waterproofing
6. **Drywall and cement board** — Cement board in wet areas, drywall in dry areas
7. **Tile** — Shower walls first, then floor
8. **Vanity and toilet** — After tile so tile goes under/behind
9. **Trim and paint** — Baseboards, door trim, paint
10. **Fixtures** — Faucets, showerhead, towel bars, mirror, light fixtures
11. **Final connections and testing**

> **Critical:** Don't skip or reorder the waterproofing step. It's the single most important part of any bathroom renovation.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- **Not getting permits.** Check with your local building department. Unpermitted work can be a problem when you sell.
- **Choosing tile before choosing the vanity.** Get the big pieces first, then select tile to complement.
- **Forgetting about storage.** A beautiful bathroom without enough storage for towels, toiletries, and cleaning supplies is frustrating to live with.
- **Skimping on lighting.** Bathrooms need good light. Plan for both overhead and vanity lighting.
- **Not having a backup bathroom.** A bathroom reno takes 2-4 weeks minimum. Make sure you have another bathroom to use.

## When to Call a Pro

For a cosmetic refresh, you can probably handle everything yourself. For anything beyond that, at minimum you'll want:

- A plumber for any plumbing changes
- An electrician for new circuits or fan installation
- A tile installer for shower walls (floor tile is more DIY-friendly)
- A general contractor if you're doing a full gut and want someone to coordinate the trades

Get at least three quotes. Ask for references and look at completed work. The cheapest bid is rarely the best value.
