## Before You Start

A leaky faucet wastes more water than most people realize — up to 3,000 gallons per year for a steady drip. The good news is that most faucet leaks are caused by worn-out parts that cost a few dollars to replace.

> **Pro Tip:** Before you start, figure out what brand and type of faucet you have. Take a photo of it and bring it to the hardware store — staff can usually help you find the right replacement parts.

## Step 1: Turn Off the Water

Find the shut-off valves under the sink and turn them clockwise until they stop. Turn on the faucet to release any remaining water pressure and confirm the water is off.

If you don't have individual shut-off valves under the sink, you'll need to shut off the main water supply to your house.

## Step 2: Plug the Drain

Put a rag or sink stopper in the drain. Small screws and washers love to disappear down open drains.

## Step 3: Identify Your Faucet Type

There are four main types:

- **Compression faucets** — Two handles, one for hot and one for cold. The most common type to leak. Uses rubber washers.
- **Ball faucets** — Single handle that moves over a rounded ball cap. Common in kitchens.
- **Cartridge faucets** — Single or double handle. Uses a cartridge that slides in and out.
- **Ceramic disc faucets** — Single handle with a wide cylinder inside. Most durable type.

## Step 4: Disassemble the Faucet

### For compression faucets:
1. Remove the decorative cap on the handle (pry gently with a flathead screwdriver)
2. Unscrew the handle screw and pull off the handle
3. Use your wrench to unscrew the packing nut
4. Pull out the stem

### For cartridge faucets:
1. Remove the handle cap and handle screw
2. Pull off the handle
3. Remove the retaining clip (if present)
4. Pull the cartridge straight up and out

## Step 5: Replace the Worn Parts

### Compression faucets:
The rubber washer at the bottom of the stem is usually the culprit. Remove the old washer (held by a brass screw) and replace it with an exact match. Apply a thin coat of plumber's grease to the new washer.

### Cartridge faucets:
Take the old cartridge to the hardware store and get an exact replacement. Slide the new one in the same orientation as the old one.

## Step 6: Reassemble and Test

Put everything back together in reverse order. Hand-tighten first, then give each connection a quarter-turn with your wrench. Don't overtighten — that causes more problems than it solves.

Turn the water back on slowly and check for leaks at every connection point.

## When to Call a Pro

- The leak is coming from the base of the faucet (could be a corroded valve seat)
- You can't identify your faucet type or find replacement parts
- The shut-off valves under the sink are stuck or leaking too
- The faucet is old and corroded — might be time for a full replacement

A plumber can replace a faucet in about an hour. If your faucet is more than 15 years old and giving you trouble, replacement is usually better value than repeated repairs.
