## Assess the Damage

The repair method depends on the size of the hole:

- **Nail holes and small dents** (under 1/2 inch) — Spackling compound only
- **Medium holes** (1/2 inch to 4 inches) — Self-adhesive mesh patch + joint compound
- **Large holes** (over 4 inches) — Drywall patch with backing support

## Small Holes: Nail Holes and Dents

This is the easiest repair and takes about 10 minutes.

1. If there's a nail or anchor still in the wall, remove it
2. Use your putty knife to press lightweight spackling compound into the hole
3. Scrape the knife flat across the surface to remove excess
4. Let it dry completely (usually 1-2 hours)
5. Sand smooth with 220-grit sandpaper
6. Prime and paint

> **Pro Tip:** Spackling shrinks slightly as it dries. For anything deeper than 1/4 inch, apply two thin coats rather than one thick one.

## Medium Holes: Up to 4 Inches

Self-adhesive mesh patches make this almost foolproof.

### Step 1: Clean the area
Remove any loose drywall or paper around the hole. Sand the area lightly to give the compound something to grip.

### Step 2: Apply the mesh patch
Peel the backing off the self-adhesive mesh patch and center it over the hole. Press firmly to make sure it sticks flat.

### Step 3: Apply joint compound
Using your 6-inch putty knife, spread a thin layer of joint compound over the entire patch. Extend at least 2 inches beyond the edges of the mesh in every direction.

### Step 4: Let it dry and repeat
Let the first coat dry completely (check the product label — usually 24 hours). Sand lightly with 120-grit, then apply a second, slightly wider coat. Let that dry, then sand smooth with 220-grit.

### Step 5: Prime and paint
Always prime a drywall repair before painting. The patched area absorbs paint differently than the surrounding wall, and without primer you'll see the repair through the paint.

## Large Holes: The California Patch

For holes bigger than 4 inches, you need actual drywall.

### Step 1: Cut a drywall patch
Cut a piece of drywall about 2 inches larger than the hole in both directions. On the back side, score and snap the drywall to the right size, but leave the front paper face 1 inch larger on all sides.

Peel away the extra drywall on the back, leaving the paper face intact as a built-in flange.

### Step 2: Trace and cut
Hold the patch over the hole and trace the drywall portion (not the paper flange) onto the wall. Cut along the traced line with a drywall saw.

### Step 3: Apply and mud
Apply a thin layer of joint compound around the hole. Press the patch into place — the paper flange sits flat against the surrounding wall. Smooth joint compound over the entire patch and flange.

### Step 4: Finish
Apply 2-3 coats of joint compound, sanding between each coat. Each coat should extend slightly wider than the last to feather the edges. Final sand with 220-grit.

## Finishing Tips

- **Match the texture.** If your walls have orange peel or knockdown texture, you'll need to replicate that. Spray-can texture from the hardware store works for small areas.
- **Paint the whole wall.** For the best color match, paint from corner to corner rather than just the patched spot.
- **Let compound dry fully.** Rushing the drying time is the number one cause of visible patches.

## When to Call a Pro

- Multiple large holes or extensive damage
- Damage near electrical outlets or plumbing (could be moisture behind the wall)
- Water-stained drywall (fix the water source first)
- Textured ceilings — ceiling work is harder than walls and more visible
